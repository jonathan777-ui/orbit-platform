3. Per-dialect cue cards

Each card: register · phonology (recognize, don't imitate) · signature words · politeness move · do / don't.

1. Mexican (Central / Standard)

  • Register: usted-forward, very courteous.
  • Phonology: clear, full -s; measured pace.
  • Signature: "ahorita," "¿mande?," "platicar," "órale," "qué padre."
  • Politeness: "¿me hace el favor de…?"
  • Do: warm, respectful, patient. Don't: rush; don't use Caribbean/Southern-Cone slang.

2. Northern Mexican (Norteño)

  • Register: usted/tú, more direct.
  • Phonology: clipped, faster than central MX.
  • Signature: "qué onda," "troca," "morro," "ándale pues."
  • Do: get to the point a touch faster. Don't: over-formalize.

3. Yucatec / Southeastern Mexican

  • Register: usted, gentle.
  • Phonology: Maya-influenced intonation, glottal touches; fuller vowels.
  • Signature: "¿qué tanto?," soft diminutives.
  • Do: warm, unhurried. Don't: imitate the intonation.

4. Cuban

  • Register: tú, warm and lively.
  • Phonology: fast; final -s aspirated/dropped ("má o meno"); -d softened.
  • Signature: "asere," "¿qué bolá?," "acere," "dale."
  • Politeness: warmth carries it; informal is fine.
  • Do: match the energy and warmth. Don't: read the dropped -s as poor Spanish — it's the norm.

5. Puerto Rican

  • Register: tú, friendly.
  • Phonology: -r→-l ("comel," "Puelto Rico"); -s aspirated; relaxed.
  • Signature: "chévere," "wepa," "nene/nena," "brutal" (= awesome).
  • Do: warm, upbeat. Don't: correct the -r/-l; expect fluid Spanglish (US PR).

6. Dominican

  • Register: tú, very warm, fast.
  • Phonology: very fast; -s frequently dropped; -r/-l/-i shifts.
  • Signature: "qué lo que" (= what's up), "vaina," "un chin" (= a little), "manito."
  • Do: keep pace, stay warm. Don't: ask them to slow/repeat more than once — confirm gently instead.

7. Central American — Voseo (GT, SV, HN, NI)

  • Register: usted for respect; vos among peers (vení, tenés, podés).
  • Phonology: softer, slower; -s sometimes aspirated (SV/HN).
  • Signature: "vaya pues," "cabal" (GT), "maje" (SV), "¿va?," "pisto" (= money).
  • Politeness: formal usted reads as respectful and is safe.
  • Do: gentle, formal-friendly. Don't: force tú if they use vos — mirror vos.

8. Costa Rican & Panamanian

  • Register: usted/vos (CR), tú/usted (PA).
  • Signature: "pura vida," "mae," "tuanis" (CR); "¿qué xopá?," "chuleta" (PA).
  • Do: relaxed warmth (CR), upbeat (PA). Don't: over-formalize CR.

9. Colombian (Andean / Bogotá)

  • Register: usted even when warm (distinctive), very courteous.
  • Phonology: clear, full -s; precise.
  • Signature: "¿me regala…?" (polite request), "sumercé" (very formal), "¿qué más?," "listo," "bacano."
  • Politeness: "¿me regala su nombre?" is the signature courteous ask.
  • Do: precise, polite, warm. Don't: drop endings; this variety values clarity.

10. Coastal Colombian & Venezuelan

  • Register: tú, lively.
  • Phonology: Caribbean-style, -s aspirated, faster (vs. Andean CO).
  • Signature: "¿qué más?," "chévere," "pana," "chamo/a" (VE), "pelao."
  • Do: warm, energetic. Don't: apply Andean formality here.

11. Andean (Peru / Ecuador / Bolivia)

  • Register: usted, measured and formal.
  • Phonology: clear -s, full vowels, even pace; Quechua/Aymara influence.
  • Signature: "¿no más?," "casero/a" (regular customer), "ya pues."
  • Do: courteous, unhurried. Don't: rush or get too casual.

12. Rioplatense (Argentina / Uruguay)

  • Register: vos (sos, tenés, querés), fairly direct.
  • Phonology: ll/y → "sh" ("sho me shamo"); Italian-like intonation.
  • Signature: "che," "dale," "bárbaro," "boludo" (peer, careful), "laburo."
  • Do: mirror vos, be direct-but-warm. Don't: use tú forms; it reads as foreign.

13. Chilean

  • Register: tú, very fast, informal.
  • Phonology: clipped endings, -s dropped, fast.
  • Signature: "po" (filler), "cachái" (= you get me), "al tiro" (= right away), "la raja."
  • Do: keep it efficient and warm. Don't: ask to repeat repeatedly — confirm key facts gently.

14. US Latino / Spanglish (sociolect)

  • Register: fluid; mirror the caller's tú/usted.
  • Phonology: US-accented Spanish + English; code-switching is normal ("una reservation," "el appointment," "el 401k," "la insurance").
  • Do: switch with them mid-sentence, keep English loanwords in English, stay natural. Don't: force "pure" Spanish — it alienates US-born callers.

15. Peninsular (Spain)

  • Register: tú/usted; vosotros for plural-you (LatAm uses ustedes).
  • Phonology: ceceo/distinción (z/c soft "th"); crisper.
  • Signature: "vale" (= okay), "tío/a" (= dude), "coger" (neutral = to take — fine in Spain).
  • Do: use only when clearly detected. Don't: apply to a US/LatAm caller.

16. Indigenous greeting layer (Rhodium premium)

  • A warm greeting + "one moment" + handoff phrase only, in the caller's indigenous language, then continue in Spanish:
  • Nahuatl: "¡Niltze!" (hello) · "Ximopanolti" (welcome).
  • K'iche'/Maya: "Saqarik" (good morning) · "Maltyox" (thank you).
  • Quechua: "¡Allillanchu!" (hello, how are you) · "Sulpayki" (thank you).
  • Yucatec Maya: "Ba'ax ka wa'alik" (hello).
  • Do: use as a respectful opener/closer only; the conversation proceeds in Spanish. Don't: attempt a full conversation in the indigenous language — capture + warm human handoff instead.

4. Universal "watch-outs" (every dialect)

  • False friends / risky words: "coger" is neutral in Spain/Argentina but vulgar in MX/Caribbean — default to "tomar"/"agarrar." "Concha" varies by region — avoid. Stay on the neutral floor for anything uncertain.
  • Numbers, money, dates: always read back in the regional cadence at Iridium+ (see voice layer §A.2); never read "$1,499.00" as digits.
  • Diminutives = warmth ("un momentito," "ahorita") — use sparingly and naturally, more in MX/Central America, less in Rioplatense.
  • When unsure of the variety → neutral floor. Never guess into slang.

End of Dialect Glossary.

5.1 Semantic traps & false friends (the high-value catches)

These cause real on-call mistakes — baked in as agent rules:

Word Safe meaning The trap Agent rule
"ahorita" "in a moment" In Mexico/Central America it's elastic — often "eventually / maybe not at all." In the Caribbean it can mean "right now." Don't rely on "ahorita" for commitments. Use precise time: "en cinco minutos," "hoy mismo," "enseguida."
"guagua" bus (CU/PR/DO + Canaries) In Chile/Ecuador/Andes "guagua" = baby/toddler. Use "guagua" for bus only in Caribbean context; say "autobús/bus" elsewhere.
"coger" to take/grab (Spain, AR, much of Caribbean) Vulgar in Mexico & most of Central America. Default to "tomar" / "agarrar" everywhere.
"concha" sweet bread (Mexico) Vulgar in AR/UY/CL/PE. Say the item by name; avoid the bare word in Southern-Cone context.
"vaina" "thing/stuff" (DR/Caribbean/CO/VE, informal) Reads too casual/edgy in formal verticals. Keep to the neutral floor ("cosa/asunto") in legal/medical/financial.
"chucho" dog (some regions) Means "cold" (Andes), "cheap/stingy," or a name elsewhere. Avoid; say "perro."
"mande" polite "pardon?/yes?" (Mexico) Warm and standard in MX; can read as subservient to some non-MX ears. Fine in MX context; elsewhere use "¿perdón? / ¿cómo dice?"

5.2 Voseo accuracy (confirmed)

  • Central American & Rioplatense voseo: vos sos · tenés · querés · podés · vení · decí · hacé — correct and interchangeable between those regions for these forms.
  • Chilean voseo (estái, tenís, querís) is non-standard / very informal — use tú in a service context, not Chilean vos.
  • Mirror vos only when the caller uses it; never initiate vos with a usted-register caller.
